Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) have transformed software development, enabling the creation of AI-powered applications known as LLM-based agents. A recent trend brings these capabilities to smartphones through mobile AI agents, which can autonomously perform complex tasks directly on the device. However, the privacy and security threats of such agents remain largely unexplored. In this paper, we take an initial step toward a systematic analysis of these risks, focusing on prompt injection attacks.

We consider threat models specific to mobile deployments, particularly vulnerabilities introduced by common operational practices such as using screenshots for perception and external APIs for control. Drawing on our experience deploying agents such as AppAgentX and mobile-agent-e within the AndroidWorld environment, we identify seven potential attack vectors and discuss practical challenges, including system protections and prompt adherence when executing complex attacker-defined tasks. Our findings refine the threat model for mobile AI agents and highlight the importance of developing more secure, self-contained on-device agents that reduce the broad attack surfaces inherent in current screenshot- and API-based designs.

Case Studies

5.1 Fraudulent Ad Injection

Agents treat on-screen ad text as instructions. Banner / app-open / notification ads inject prompts that divert workflows (e.g., in Notes), inducing clicks and unintended navigation.

5.2 Phishing via Fake Login

Malicious links/ad redirects present look-alike pages; the agent auto-enters credentials or continues work on a fake note page, leaking sensitive data.

5.4 System Data Discovery

5.6 Malicious App Deployment

Ad-driven diversion to store page leads the agent to install/open an app and grant permissions, yielding persistence and privilege expansion.

5.7 Malware Deployment (Sideload)

Browser download with warnings bypassed and an installer chain completes sideloading; malware can execute and connect to remote control.
